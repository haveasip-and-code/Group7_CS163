pierce
1. To thrust into, penetrate, or transfix, with a pointed instrument. \"I pierce . . . her tender side.\" Dryden. 2. To penetrate; to enter; to force a way into or through; to pass into or through; as, to pierce the enemy's line; a shot pierced the ship. 3. Fig.: To penetrate; to affect deeply; as, to pierce a mystery. \"Pierced with grief.\" Pope. Can no prayers pierce thee Shak.\n\nTo enter; to penetrate; to make a way into or through something, as a pointed instrument does; -- used literally and figuratively. And pierced to the skin, but bit no more. Spenser. She would not pierce further into his meaning. Sir P. Sidney.
