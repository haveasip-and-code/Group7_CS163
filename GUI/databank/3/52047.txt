droop
1. To hang bending downward; to sink or hang down, as an animal, plant, etc., from physical inability or exhaustion, want of nourishment, or the like. \"The purple flowers droop.\" \"Above her drooped a lamp.\" Tennyson. I saw him ten days before he died, and observed he began very much to droop and languish. Swift. 2. To grow weak or faint with disappointment, grief, or like causes; to be dispirited or depressed; to languish; as, her spirits drooped. I'll animate the soldier's drooping courage. Addison. 3. To proceed downward, or toward a close; to decline. \"Then day drooped.\" Tennyson.\n\nTo let droop or sink. [R.] M. Arnold. Like to a withered vine That droops his sapless branches to the ground. Shak.\n\nA drooping; as, a droop of the eye.
