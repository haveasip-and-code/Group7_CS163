chink
A small cleft, rent, or fissure, of greater length than breadth; a gap or crack; as, the chinks of wall. Through one cloudless chink, in a black, stormy sky. Shines out the dewy morning star. Macaulay.\n\nTo crack; to open.\n\n1. To cause to open in cracks or fissures. 2. To fill up the chinks of; as, to chink a wall.\n\n1. A short, sharp sound, as of metal struck with a slight degree of violence. \"Chink of bell.\" Cowper. 2. Money; cash. [Cant] \"To leave his chink to better hands.\" Somerville.\n\nTo cause to make a sharp metallic sound, as coins, small pieces of metal, etc., by bringing them into collision with each other. Pope.\n\nTo make a slight, sharp, metallic sound, as by the collision of little pieces of money, or other small sonorous bodies. Arbuthnot.
