The infusion has a pleasant odor, with an agreeable bitter taste, and is much used for tea in South America.\n\nSame as Checkmate.\n\nSee 2d Mat. [Obs.] Chaucer.\n\n1. To confuse; to confound. [Obs.] Shak. 2. To checkmate.\n\n1. One who customarily associates with another; a companion; an associate; any object which is associated or combined with a similar object. 2. Hence, specifically, a husband or wife; and among the lower animals, one of a pair associated for propagation and the care of their young. 3. A suitable companion; a match; an equal. Ye knew me once no mate For you; there sitting where you durst not soar. Milton. 4. (Naut.)  An officer in a merchant vessel ranking next below the captain. If there are more than one bearing the title, they are called, respectively, first mate, second mate, third mate, etc. In the navy, a subordinate officer or assistant; as, master's mate; surgeon's mate.\n\n1. To match; to marry. If she be mated with an equal husband. Shak. 2. To match one's self against; to oppose as equal; to compete with. There is no passion in the mind of man so weak but it mates and masters the fear of death. Bacon. I, . . . in the way of loyalty and truth, . . . Dare mate a sounder man than Surrey can be. Shak.\n\nTo be or become a mate or mates, especially in sexual companionship; as, some birds mate for life; this bird will not mate with that one.
